SECTION 1: WHAT IS A FAKE JOB OFFER SCAM?
================================================================================

A fake job offer scam is a type of fraud where criminals post fake job advertisements
or send unsolicited job offers to attract victims with promises of high-paying or
easy work. Once a victim expresses interest, scammers deceive them into:
- Paying upfront "registration" or "training" fees
- Revealing personal information (IC, passport, bank account) for identity theft
- Performing illegal tasks unknowingly (becoming money mules)
- Being trafficked to another country to run scam operations

In Malaysia, fake job scams have reached alarming levels. The Ministry of Human
Resources (MOHR) and JTKSM (Jabatan Tenaga Kerja Semenanjung Malaysia) have issued
multiple public warnings. PDRM has reported thousands of Malaysians trafficked to
Cambodia, Myanmar, and the Philippines by criminal syndicates operating under the
guise of job offers.

High-risk groups: Fresh graduates, job seekers, those facing financial difficulties,
students looking for part-time work, and Malaysians seeking work abroad.

SECTION 2: HOW FAKE JOB OFFER SCAMS WORK (STEP BY STEP)
================================================================================

STEP 1 - JOB ADVERTISEMENT OR DIRECT APPROACH
Scammer posts a fake job ad on:
- Jobstreet, LinkedIn, Indeed (fake accounts)
- Facebook groups and marketplace
- Telegram and WhatsApp groups
- Instagram and TikTok
OR scammer directly contacts victim via WhatsApp/Telegram with an unsolicited offer.

Typical fake job descriptions:
- "Work from home data entry — earn RM300-RM500 per day"
- "Part-time social media assistant — RM5,000/month"
- "Online product reviewer — RM200 per task"
- "Customer service officer in Bangkok/Singapore — RM8,000/month"
- "Crypto trading assistant — high earnings, training provided"

STEP 2 - BUILDING TRUST
Scammer presents convincing materials: fake company website, professional logos,
fake employee testimonials, fake offer letters on letterhead. They may conduct
a short "interview" via Zoom or WhatsApp. Victim is told they are "selected."

STEP 3 - UPFRONT FEES (for fee-based scam variant)
After "hiring" the victim, scammer requests payment for:
- Registration fees ("processing your application")
- Training materials or course fees
- Work permit / visa fees (for overseas job scams)
- Uniform, equipment, or ID card fees
- Background check fees
After payment, the job never materialises. Contact is cut off.

STEP 4 - TASK-BASED SCAM (for online part-time job variant)
Victim is given "tasks" such as:
- Liking YouTube videos or following social media accounts
- Submitting fake product reviews
- "Boosting" app ratings
Victim earns small amounts initially. Then told they need to pay a deposit to
unlock bigger commissions. After paying, the platform freezes and they lose access.

STEP 5 - MONEY MULE RECRUITMENT
Victim is asked to receive money into their bank account and transfer it to another
account, keeping a small percentage. Victim unknowingly becomes a money mule.
This is illegal — even if the victim did not know the funds were from crime.
Consequences: criminal charges, frozen accounts, AKPK records.

STEP 6 - HUMAN TRAFFICKING (WORST CASE)
Victim is offered a high-paying tech or customer service job overseas (Cambodia,
Myanmar, Thailand, Philippines). After arrival, passport is confiscated.
Victim is forced to work for criminal scam centres. They are made to scam others
online. Victims who resist face violence, debt bondage, or ransom demands.

SECTION 3: TYPES OF FAKE JOB SCAMS
================================================================================

TYPE 1 - WORK FROM HOME / ONLINE PART-TIME SCAM (Most Common in Malaysia)
"Easy tasks, big earnings" — like reviews, clicks, social media likes.
Initial small earnings build trust. Then victim must deposit money to "unlock"
bigger tasks or commissions. After depositing, platform disappears.
Key identifier: Any job that requires you to DEPOSIT money to EARN money is a scam.

TYPE 2 - UPFRONT FEE JOB SCAM
Victim is "hired" but must pay registration, training, or equipment fees first.
Fee ranges from RM100 to RM5,000. Job never materialises after payment.
Key identifier: Legitimate employers do not charge candidates any fees.

TYPE 3 - OVERSEAS JOB SCAM / HUMAN TRAFFICKING
High-paying job in Southeast Asia with free flights and accommodation provided.
After arrival, victim is trapped and forced to work for scam syndicates.
Key identifier: Legitimate overseas jobs are arranged through registered recruiters
and involve formal employment contracts, not WhatsApp job offers.

TYPE 4 - MONEY MULE RECRUITMENT
Victim is paid a commission to receive and forward money through their personal
bank account. Victim is unaware the money is from scam victims.
Key identifier: Any arrangement to use your personal bank account to receive and
forward payments is illegal, regardless of whether you were deceived.

TYPE 5 - FAKE INVESTMENT TRAINER / MENTOR JOB
Victim is "hired" as an investment or crypto trading assistant and given access
to a fake trading platform. They are encouraged to invest their own money to
"demonstrate skills." Platform eventually freezes and money disappears.
Key identifier: No legitimate trading job requires employees to invest their own funds.

TYPE 6 - SOCIAL MEDIA INFLUENCER / MODEL CASTING SCAM
Victim is told they are selected for a modelling contract or influencer deal.
They are asked to pay a registration fee or purchase products for "review."
Key identifier: Legitimate talent agencies do not charge upfront fees.

SECTION 4: RED FLAGS AND WARNING SIGNS
================================================================================

TOO GOOD TO BE TRUE:
- Very high pay for simple or easy tasks (RM500/day for clicking buttons)
- No experience or qualifications required for high-paying positions
- Salary far above market rate for the type of work described
- "Guaranteed income" or "fixed monthly earning" regardless of performance

SUSPICIOUS JOB POSTING:
- Job ad is posted by a new or recently created account with few followers
- Company name cannot be found in SSM (Companies Commission of Malaysia) registry
- Company website was registered recently (check whois.domaintools.com)
- Contact information leads to only a WhatsApp number or personal email
- Job description is vague — no clear company name, physical address, or job scope

UPFRONT PAYMENT REQUESTS:
- Any request for payment before you start work — for any reason
- Payment for registration, training, equipment, ID card, uniform, or visa fees
- Told to pay a "refundable deposit" to start earning
- Payment must be made urgently within 24–48 hours

PRESSURE AND URGENCY:
- "We need to fill this position immediately — decide now"
- "Only 3 spots left — you must pay registration today"
- Pressure to make quick decisions without time to verify or consult others

COMMUNICATION RED FLAGS:
- Contacted out of nowhere via WhatsApp or Telegram (unsolicited job offer)
- Interviewer refuses to do a video call or meet in person
- All communication happens only on personal WhatsApp, not company channels
- "HR manager" uses a personal Gmail or Yahoo email instead of company domain
- Recruiter uses broken English or unnatural phrasing

OVERSEAS JOB RED FLAGS:
- Recruiter arranges everything including visa and flight — too convenient
- Job location is Cambodia, Myanmar, Thailand, Philippines (high-trafficking risk areas)
- Recruiter says passport will be kept "for safekeeping" — NEVER allow this
- No formal employment contract provided
- Cannot verify the company exists in the destination country
- Contact disappears after you arrive at destination

SECTION 5: RISK ASSESSMENT RULES (IF-THEN PRODUCTION RULES)
================================================================================

RULE JS-01 [HIGH / CRITICAL RISK]:
IF user is offered an overseas job AND the job is located in Cambodia, Myanmar,
Thailand, or Philippines AND the recruiter arranged the flight and accommodation
AND user has not seen a formal employment contract THEN risk level is HIGH. If
the user is already travelling, has arrived, is being confined, threatened, or
has lost control of travel documents, THEN risk level is CRITICAL.
ACTION: Do NOT travel. This is a human trafficking red flag. Report to JTKSM
and PDRM immediately.

RULE JS-02 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user is asked to pay ANY fee before starting work THEN risk level is HIGH.
ACTION: Stop all communication. Legitimate employers never charge job seekers.
Report to JTKSM at www.jtksm.gov.my.

RULE JS-03 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user was offered a job via unsolicited WhatsApp message AND the job promises
high income for simple online tasks THEN risk level is HIGH.
ACTION: Do NOT engage further. This is a classic online part-time scam entry point.

RULE JS-04 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user is asked to receive money into their personal bank account and forward it
to another account in exchange for commission THEN risk level is HIGH. If the
user already received, transferred, or allowed their account to be used for this
money movement, THEN risk level is CRITICAL.
ACTION: Do NOT agree to this under any circumstances. This may be money mule
activity and can become a criminal offence under Section 4 of AMLATFPUAA 2001.

RULE JS-05 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user completed initial tasks and earned small amounts AND is now being asked
to deposit money to "unlock" higher-earning tasks THEN risk level is HIGH. If
the user already deposited money or is being pressured to pay more to recover
funds, THEN risk level is CRITICAL.
ACTION: Stop immediately. The platform is a scam — deposited money will not be returned.

RULE JS-06 [MEDIUM RISK]:
IF user found a job offer from a company whose name cannot be verified in SSM
records AND the offer sounds unusually attractive THEN risk level is MEDIUM.
ACTION: Verify company on SSM website (ssm.com.my) before proceeding.
Ask for a formal interview in person at a physical office.

RULE JS-07 [LOW RISK - AWARENESS]:
IF user wants to verify whether a job offer is legitimate THEN provide verification
checklist without immediately labelling as scam.
ACTION: Guide user to check company registration, verify through official platforms,
avoid upfront payments, and consult JTKSM if unsure.

SECTION 6: HOW TO VERIFY A JOB OFFER IS LEGITIMATE
================================================================================

STEP 1 - VERIFY COMPANY REGISTRATION:
Check if the company is registered with SSM (Companies Commission of Malaysia):
Visit ssm.com.my — search the company name. All legitimate companies must be
registered. If the company is not found, it is likely fake.

STEP 2 - VERIFY EMPLOYER / RECRUITER:
For overseas job placements, check if the recruitment agency is registered with JTKSM.
Visit: jtksm.gov.my — look up registered overseas employment agencies.
Only use recruitment agencies licensed by JTKSM for overseas jobs.

STEP 3 - VERIFY JOB PLATFORM:
Jobstreet, LinkedIn, and Indeed have verification processes, but scammers can create
fake accounts. Cross-reference by searching for the company independently — not
through the link in the job post.

STEP 4 - VERIFY PHYSICAL ADDRESS:
Does the company have a real physical address? Search on Google Maps. Does the
location exist? Call the company using the number from their official website
(not the number from the job posting).

STEP 5 - GOOGLE THE JOB OFFER:
Copy a unique sentence from the job description and paste it in Google with the
word "scam". Scam job descriptions are often copy-pasted and used repeatedly.
Search for the company name + "scam" or "review" to find victim reports.

STEP 6 - NEVER PAY TO WORK:
This is the single most reliable rule. No matter how convincing the job sounds —
if they ask for money before you start working, it is a scam. Period.

SECTION 7: MONEY MULE — KNOW THE LAW
================================================================================

What is a money mule?
A money mule is a person who transfers illegally obtained money through their
personal bank account on behalf of others, knowingly or unknowingly.

Is it a crime even if you didn't know?
YES. Under the Anti-Money Laundering, Anti-Terrorism Financing and Proceeds of
Unlawful Activities Act (AMLATFPUAA) 2001, Section 4:
- Conviction for money laundering carries a fine of up to RM5,000,000 OR
  imprisonment of up to 15 years, or BOTH.
- "Not knowing" is a weak defence — courts consider whether you should have known.
- Your bank account will be permanently blacklisted, affecting future banking.

How to avoid becoming a money mule:
- Never allow anyone to use your bank account to receive or transfer money
- Never share your bank account details with people you met online
- Never accept commissions for transferring money through your account
- If approached, report the incident to PDRM and your bank immediately

SECTION 8: WHAT TO DO IF YOU ARE A VICTIM
================================================================================

IF YOU PAID AN UPFRONT FEE:
1. Stop all communication with the scammer
2. Report to PDRM: ccid.rmp.gov.my or nearest police station
3. Call NSRC 997 if payment was made recently (they may freeze the mule account)
4. Contact your bank to dispute the transaction
5. Report the fake job posting to the platform (Jobstreet, Facebook, etc.)

IF YOU ARE STRANDED OVERSEAS (Trafficking Victim):
1. Contact Malaysian Embassy or High Commission in the country
2. Call Wisma Putra Hotline: 03-8887-4000 (24 hours)
3. Contact PDRM via family members in Malaysia
4. Contact Interpol Malaysia via PDRM
5. Do NOT give in to ransom demands — contact official channels first

IF YOU WERE USED AS A MONEY MULE (even unknowingly):
1. Report yourself to PDRM proactively — voluntary reporting shows good faith
2. Contact your bank immediately to explain the situation
3. Consult a lawyer — you may need legal representation
4. File a police report to document that you were deceived

REPORTING:
- PDRM (CCID): ccid.rmp.gov.my | 03-2610-1559

- Wisma Putra (Malaysians stranded abroad): 03-8887-4000
