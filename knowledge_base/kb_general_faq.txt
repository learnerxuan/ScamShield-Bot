SECTION 1: GENERAL FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS (FAQ)
================================================================================

Q: I think I am being scammed. What should I do first?
A: Stay calm and STOP. Do not send any more money, do not provide any more personal
information, and do not install any more apps. Cut off all communication with the
suspected scammer. If money has been transferred recently, call NSRC 997 immediately —
this is the fastest way to attempt to freeze the mule account before the scammer
withdraws the money.

---

Q: How do I know if something is a scam?
A: Common warning signs across all scam types:
1. Unsolicited contact — you did not initiate the communication
2. Too good to be true — unrealistically high earnings, prizes, or returns
3. Urgency and pressure — must act NOW or lose money / face consequences
4. Requests for money upfront — pay fees before receiving anything
5. Requests for OTP or banking credentials — never share these with anyone
6. Secrecy — told to keep the matter confidential or not tell family
7. Payment via gift cards, cryptocurrency, or untraceable methods
If two or more of these signs are present, treat it as a scam until proven otherwise.

---

Q: My friend referred me to this investment/job. Can it still be a scam?
A: Yes, unfortunately. Your friend may themselves be a victim who was deceived into
recruiting others — this is common in Ponzi schemes, fake investment platforms, and
online part-time scams. Your friend is not necessarily at fault. Independently verify
any opportunity through SC Malaysia, SSM, or JTKSM before committing funds or time.

---

Q: I already sent money. Can I get it back?
A: Speed is critical. The moment you realise you have been scammed:
1. Call NSRC 997 — they coordinate with banks to freeze mule accounts
2. Call your bank's fraud hotline to dispute the transaction
3. File a police report immediately
Recovery is possible in some cases if the mule account is frozen before the money
is withdrawn. However, once funds reach the final scammer's account (often overseas),
recovery is very difficult. Acting within minutes to hours gives the best chance.

---

Q: Is there a single number I can call for all scam types?
A: Yes — call NSRC (National Scam Response Centre): 997
Operating hours: 8:00 AM to 8:00 PM, 7 days a week.
This is Malaysia's dedicated anti-scam hotline that coordinates with all major banks.
For emergencies outside NSRC hours: call PDRM at 999 or your bank's 24-hour fraud line.

---

Q: I am embarrassed to report — will people judge me?
A: Scam victims come from all backgrounds, education levels, and ages. These criminals
are professional manipulators who study psychology and exploit human trust and emotions.
Being scammed is not a reflection of intelligence — it is the result of calculated
deception by criminal syndicates. Reporting helps prevent the same criminals from
victimising others. PDRM and NSRC officers handle these cases with confidentiality
and professionalism.

---

Q: Can I go to jail for being used as a money mule without knowing?
A: Potentially yes, though voluntary reporting significantly reduces risk.
Under AMLATFPUAA 2001, allowing your bank account to be used for money laundering
is a criminal offence even if you were deceived. If you discover you have been used
as a mule: stop immediately, go to PDRM voluntarily to report, and consult a lawyer.
Proactive reporting demonstrates good faith and is taken into consideration.

---

Q: How do I check if an investment is legitimate in Malaysia?
A: Three steps:
1. Check SC Malaysia's registered and licensed entities list: sc.com.my
2. Check BNM's Financial Consumer Alert (unlicensed entities): bnm.gov.my
3. Check SC Malaysia's InvestSmart investor alert list: investsmart.sc.com.my
If the investment operator is not on the licensed list OR is on the alert/warning list,
do not invest.

---

Q: How do I check if a company offering me a job is legitimate?
A: Check SSM (Companies Commission of Malaysia): ssm.com.my
All legitimate companies operating in Malaysia must be registered with SSM.
For overseas jobs: verify the recruitment agency at JTKSM: jtksm.gov.my
Only use JTKSM-registered agencies for overseas employment arrangements.

---

Q: Someone sent me money I wasn't expecting and asked me to forward it. Should I?
A: NO. This is how money mules are recruited. The money you received is stolen from
scam victims. Forwarding it makes you part of the money laundering chain.
This is illegal under AMLATFPUAA 2001 regardless of whether you knew the source.
Return the money to your bank and report the request to PDRM immediately.

---

Q: My bank account has been frozen. What do I do?
A: Your account may have been flagged by police or PDRM as a mule account — sometimes
this happens to genuine victims who received suspicious transfers unknowingly.
Steps to take:
1. Contact your bank to understand the reason for the freeze
2. If freeze is related to a criminal investigation, you will receive a notice
3. Consult a lawyer to assist with unfreezing your account
4. Report your side of the story to PDRM to establish you are a victim, not a mule

---

Q: What is the NSRC and how does it work?
A: NSRC (National Scam Response Centre) is Malaysia's centralised anti-scam coordination
centre. It was established by PDRM and works with all major Malaysian banks.
When you call 997 after being scammed, NSRC officers can:
- Coordinate with banks to freeze mule accounts in real time
- Escalate urgent cases to prevent further withdrawals
- Refer cases to PDRM for criminal investigation
NSRC operates 8:00 AM to 8:00 PM daily. For urgent cases after hours, call your
bank's 24-hour fraud hotline directly.

---

Q: Can I report a scam if I did not lose any money?
A: YES — and please do. Reporting attempted scams helps PDRM and MCMC build intelligence
on active scam syndicates, shut down fake websites, and warn the public before more
victims lose money. Report at:
- MCMC: aduan.mcmc.gov.my (for online/messaging scams)
- PDRM: ccid.rmp.gov.my (for cybercrime reports)
- NSRC: 997 (for all scam types)
Even if you only received a suspicious SMS or call, reporting it is valuable.

---

Q: A friend is being scammed but won't listen to me. What can I do?
A: Romance scam and investment scam victims often develop strong emotional bonds with
their scammers and may dismiss warnings from friends and family.
What you can do:
1. Stay patient and non-judgmental — do not tell them they are stupid
2. Present factual information calmly — red flags from this chatbot or official sources
3. Encourage them to do a reverse image search or call a trusted financial advisor
4. Contact PDRM or MCMC and report your concern — authorities can sometimes intervene
5. Reach out to the Malaysian Scam Victims Support community on social media
6. If the person is at financial risk, consider involving other family members

SECTION 2: QUICK RISK SELF-ASSESSMENT
================================================================================

Answer YES or NO to each question. Count your YES answers.

1. Did someone contact you out of nowhere with this offer, investment, or relationship?
2. Does the offer promise very high returns, income, or prizes for little effort?
3. Are you being asked to act urgently within hours or days?
4. Have you been asked to pay any fee upfront before receiving anything?
5. Have you been asked to share your OTP, banking password, or PIN?
6. Have you been told to keep this matter secret from family or friends?
7. Does the person or company offer cannot be verified on official government websites?
8. Are you being asked to receive money and forward it to another account?
9. Have you been asked to send gift cards, cryptocurrency, or wire transfers?
10. Does something about this feel "too good to be true"?

RESULTS:
0 YES answers: Low risk — but stay vigilant
1-2 YES answers: Moderate concern — verify the situation before proceeding
3-4 YES answers: High risk — stop and verify with official sources before any action
5+ YES answers: Very high risk — this is very likely a scam. Stop all activity.
               Call NSRC 997 or PDRM 03-2610-1559 for assistance.

SECTION 3: POST-INCIDENT STEP-BY-STEP GUIDE
================================================================================

IF YOU JUST REALISED YOU MAY HAVE BEEN SCAMMED:
Follow these steps in order — every minute counts.

--- WITHIN THE FIRST 30 MINUTES ---

STEP 1 - STOP ALL ACTIVITY
Stop sending money immediately. Do not send anything more regardless of what the
scammer says — threats, emotional appeals, or promises will not change the outcome.

STEP 2 - CALL NSRC 997
Tell them: how much you sent, when you sent it, and which bank or method you used.
They can coordinate with banks in real time to freeze mule accounts.
NSRC hotline: 997 (available 8 AM to 8 PM daily)

STEP 3 - CALL YOUR BANK'S FRAUD HOTLINE
Request an immediate account freeze or transaction dispute.
Provide the transaction date, amount, and destination account if known.
Bank fraud hotlines (24-hour):
- Maybank: 1-300-88-6688
- CIMB: 1-300-880-900
- Public Bank: 1-800-22-5555
- RHB: 1-800-88-9950
- Hong Leong: 1-300-88-8111
- AmBank: 1-800-88-9999
- BSN: 1-300-88-1900
- Bank Islam: 1-300-88-2265
- Bank Rakyat: 1-800-88-5565

STEP 4 - SECURE YOUR ACCOUNTS
If you shared passwords or banking credentials:
- Change all passwords from a different, clean device
- Enable two-factor authentication on all accounts
- Contact your bank to change your banking PIN
- If your email was compromised — recovery may require contacting the email provider

--- WITHIN 24 HOURS ---

STEP 5 - PRESERVE EVIDENCE
Screenshot everything: messages, transaction receipts, profile photos, platform
screenshots, phone numbers, email addresses. Do NOT delete any conversations.
This evidence is needed for the police report and bank dispute.

STEP 6 - FILE A POLICE REPORT
Option A: Online report at ccid.rmp.gov.my (for cybercrime)
Option B: Walk into any nearest police station and file report in person
Bring with you: IC, screenshots, transaction receipts, scammer's contact details
You will receive a police report reference number — keep this for all future follow-up.

STEP 7 - REPORT TO RELEVANT AUTHORITIES
Depending on scam type, also report to:
- SC Malaysia (investment scams): sc.com.my or 1-800-88-5465
- MCMC (online/messaging scams): aduan.mcmc.gov.my or 1-800-188-030
- BNM (banking-related fraud): bnmtelelink@bnm.gov.my or 1-300-88-5465
- MyCERT (malware/device compromise): mycert.org.my or +603-8992-6969
- JTKSM (job/labour scams): jtksm.gov.my or 03-8886-5000

--- WITHIN THE FIRST WEEK ---

STEP 8 - FOLLOW UP WITH BANK
Ask for the outcome of the dispute. Request a formal written response.
If dissatisfied with your bank's response, escalate to:
- Ombudsman for Financial Services (OFS): ofs.org.my | 03-2272-2811
- BNM BNMTELELINK: 1-300-88-5465

STEP 9 - CONSULT FINANCIAL COUNSELLING (IF NEEDED)
If you took loans or are in debt due to the scam:
- AKPK (Agensi Kaunseling dan Pengurusan Kredit): 1-800-88-2575 | akpk.org.my
  Free service for Malaysians in debt difficulty

STEP 10 - SEEK EMOTIONAL SUPPORT
Being scammed is traumatic. You do not have to cope alone:
- Befrienders Malaysia: 03-7627-2929 (24 hours)
- Talian Kasih: 15999 or WhatsApp 019-261-5999
- Malaysian Mental Health Association: 03-2780-6803

SECTION 5: SCAM AWARENESS — KNOW YOUR RIGHTS
================================================================================

RIGHT 1 - RIGHT TO REFUSE:
You have the absolute right to refuse any request for money, OTP, or personal
information from any person, whether they claim to be a bank, government officer,
courier, employer, or romantic partner. No legitimate institution will threaten
you with arrest or account closure for refusing to comply immediately.

RIGHT 2 - RIGHT TO VERIFY:
You have the right to hang up and call back using the official number before
taking any action. No legitimate organisation will prevent you from independently
verifying their identity. A caller who objects to you hanging up to verify is a scammer.

RIGHT 3 - RIGHT TO REPORT:
You have the right to report any suspicious activity to PDRM, MCMC, SC Malaysia,
or BNM without fear of consequences. Reporting a scam — even if no money was lost —
is your civic duty and helps protect others in your community.

RIGHT 4 - RIGHT TO DISPUTE:
Under BNM's consumer protection guidelines, you have the right to file a formal
dispute with your bank for unauthorised transactions. Banks are required to investigate
and respond in writing. If unsatisfied, you can escalate to OFS or BNM BNMTELELINK.

RIGHT 5 - RIGHT TO PROTECTION:
Malaysia has multiple laws protecting citizens from financial fraud:
- Computer Crimes Act 1997
- Communications and Multimedia Act 1998
- Anti-Money Laundering, Anti-Terrorism Financing and Proceeds of Unlawful
  Activities Act (AMLATFPUAA) 2001
- Capital Markets and Services Act (CMSA) 2007
- Financial Services Act (FSA) 2013
- Personal Data Protection Act (PDPA) 2010

SECTION 6: KEY SCAM PREVENTION RULES TO REMEMBER
================================================================================

THE GOLDEN RULES OF SCAM PREVENTION:

RULE 1 — NEVER share your OTP with anyone. Ever.
Your OTP is the last line of defence for your bank account. No bank officer,
government agent, family member, or romantic partner has a legitimate reason
to ask for it.

RULE 2 — NEVER pay upfront to receive something.
Whether it is a job, a prize, an investment return, or a parcel — if you have
to pay before receiving, stop. Legitimate rewards do not require upfront payments.

RULE 3 — NEVER transfer money to a safe account.
No bank, government agency, or police force will ever ask you to transfer money
to a different account to protect it from fraud. This is always a scam.

RULE 4 — NEVER install an app from a link in a message.
All legitimate apps are available on the official Google Play Store or Apple App Store.
APK files sent via WhatsApp, Telegram, or SMS are almost always malware.

RULE 5 — VERIFY before you trust, click, or pay.
When in doubt: hang up, do not click, do not pay. Verify independently using
official websites or official hotline numbers. Scammers rely on urgency to prevent
you from stopping to verify.

RULE 6 — TALK to someone you trust.
Scammers thrive on isolation and secrecy. Before making any significant financial
decision based on an online interaction, discuss it with a trusted family member
or friend. A fresh perspective often reveals what you cannot see when under pressure.

RULE 7 — REPORT everything — even if you lost nothing.
Every report helps build intelligence against scam syndicates.
