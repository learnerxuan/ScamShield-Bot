SECTION 1: WHAT IS PHISHING?
================================================================================

Phishing is a type of cybercrime where scammers impersonate trusted organisations
such as banks, government agencies, courier services, or popular websites to trick
victims into revealing sensitive personal information. This includes passwords,
banking credentials, credit card numbers, MyKad/IC numbers, and OTP codes.

In Malaysia, phishing is one of the most reported cybercrimes. MyCERT and MCMC
receive thousands of phishing reports annually. Phishing attacks target all
demographics — students, working adults, senior citizens, and businesses.

Common impersonated entities in Malaysia:
- Banks: Maybank, CIMB, Public Bank, RHB, Hong Leong, AmBank, BSN
- Government: LHDN (tax refund), JPJ, PDRM, KWSP/EPF, MySejahtera
- E-commerce: Shopee, Lazada, Grab, Touch 'n Go eWallet
- Telcos: Maxis, Celcom, Digi, TM Unifi
- Delivery: Pos Malaysia, J&T Express, DHL, FedEx

SECTION 2: HOW PHISHING SCAMS WORK (STEP BY STEP)
================================================================================

STEP 1 - INITIAL CONTACT
The scammer sends a message via SMS, WhatsApp, email, or social media. The message
appears to come from a legitimate source. It creates urgency, fear, or excitement.
Examples: "Your account will be suspended", "You have a pending tax refund of RM1,200",
"Congratulations! You won a prize."

STEP 2 - FAKE LINK OR ATTACHMENT
The message contains a link to a fake website that looks identical to the real one.
OR it contains a malicious attachment (PDF, Word document) that installs malware.
The fake website URL often looks similar but is slightly different:
- Real: www.maybank2u.com.my
- Fake: www.maybank2u-secure.com / www.maybonk2u.com / www.maybank.com.my-login.info

STEP 3 - CREDENTIAL HARVESTING
Victim enters username, password, IC number, banking PIN, or OTP on the fake site.
The scammer immediately captures all entered information in real time.

STEP 4 - ACCOUNT TAKEOVER OR IDENTITY THEFT
Using the stolen credentials, the scammer:
- Logs into the victim's real bank account and transfers money
- Takes over the victim's social media or email accounts
- Applies for loans or credit cards under the victim's identity
- Sells the stolen data on underground markets

STEP 5 - MONEY LOSS
Victims typically realise they have been scammed only after noticing unauthorised
transactions on their bank statement or when they are locked out of their accounts.

SECTION 3: TYPES OF PHISHING
================================================================================

TYPE 1 - EMAIL PHISHING (Most common)
Bulk emails sent to thousands of victims at once. Claims to be from banks, LHDN,
government agencies, or popular services. Contains urgent call to action.
Red flag: Generic greeting ("Dear Customer" instead of your actual name).

TYPE 2 - SMS PHISHING (Smishing)
Fraudulent text messages with shortened URLs (e.g., bit.ly, tinyurl). Very common
in Malaysia — often impersonates Pos Malaysia, banks, or government agencies.
Red flag: Unsolicited SMS with a link asking you to verify account details.

TYPE 3 - VOICE PHISHING (Vishing)
Phone calls from scammers pretending to be bank officers, police, LHDN officers,
or Telekom Malaysia. They create urgency ("your account is frozen", "you have a
warrant of arrest"). They may ask you to press keys on your keypad or reveal OTPs.
Red flag: Caller asks for OTP, banking PIN, or IC number over the phone.

TYPE 4 - SPEAR PHISHING
Highly targeted phishing that uses personal information to appear more convincing.
The email or message addresses you by name, mentions your employer, or references
recent transactions to gain your trust. Common in corporate environments.
Red flag: Personalised message from unknown sender asking for urgent action.

TYPE 5 - WHALING
Spear phishing that specifically targets high-level executives, CEOs, or finance
managers. Often requests urgent wire transfers or asks executives to approve
fraudulent invoices. Also known as Business Email Compromise (BEC).
Red flag: CEO or senior manager's email asking for an urgent fund transfer.

TYPE 6 - CLONE PHISHING
Scammer duplicates a legitimate email you previously received but replaces the
links or attachments with malicious ones. The sender address is spoofed.
Red flag: A follow-up email claiming to resend a previous attachment.

TYPE 7 - PHARMING
Victim is redirected to a fake website even when typing the correct URL. Malware
modifies the victim's computer or router DNS settings.
Red flag: Bank website looks slightly different, SSL padlock is missing.

SECTION 4: RED FLAGS AND WARNING SIGNS
================================================================================

URGENCY AND PRESSURE:
- "Your account will be blocked in 24 hours if you do not verify now"
- "You must act immediately to avoid legal action"
- "Offer expires today — claim your reward now"
- Messages that create panic, fear, or extreme excitement

SUSPICIOUS SENDER:
- Email sender domain does not match official company domain
  Example: maybank@gmail.com (FAKE) vs noreply@maybank.com.my (REAL)
- Slight misspellings in sender address (mayb4nk, maybonk, m4ybank)
- Caller ID shows overseas number (+1, +44, +62) for a local bank or agency

SUSPICIOUS LINK:
- URL contains IP address instead of domain name (e.g., http://192.168.1.1/login)
- URL uses HTTP instead of HTTPS (missing security padlock)
- URL domain is slightly different from official:
  e.g., bnm-gov.com.my instead of bnm.gov.my
- URL uses free hosting: .wixsite.com, .weebly.com, .blogspot.com
- Shortened URLs in SMS or email (bit.ly, tinyurl, goo.gl) hiding the real destination
- Pop-up warning appears when you hover over the link

REQUESTS FOR SENSITIVE INFORMATION:
- Any request for OTP (One-Time Password) — real banks NEVER ask for OTP
- Request for full password or PIN over phone, SMS, or email
- Request to share your screen via AnyDesk, TeamViewer, or similar apps
- Request for IC number, passport number, or date of birth via message

POOR QUALITY CONTENT:
- Spelling errors, grammatical mistakes, or awkward phrasing in official-looking messages
- Mismatched logos, low-quality images, or outdated branding
- Generic greetings ("Dear User", "Dear Customer") instead of your name

UNUSUAL REQUESTS:
- Ask to install a new app for "security purposes" (often RAT malware)
- Ask to withdraw cash and deposit it into a specified account
- Ask to purchase gift cards or e-wallet top-ups as payment
- Ask to take a photo of your bank card (front and back)

SECTION 5: RISK ASSESSMENT RULES (IF-THEN PRODUCTION RULES)
================================================================================

RULE P-01 [CRITICAL RISK]:
IF user received a message AND the message contains a link AND the link leads to
a page requesting OTP or banking password THEN risk level is CRITICAL.
ACTION: Do NOT enter any information. Close the page. Report to MyCERT immediately.

RULE P-02 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user received an SMS or WhatsApp from unknown number AND message claims to be
from a bank AND message contains a link THEN risk level is HIGH.
ACTION: Do NOT click the link. Verify directly with the bank by calling official hotline.

RULE P-03 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user received a phone call AND caller claims to be a bank officer or police officer
AND caller asks for OTP or PIN THEN risk level is HIGH.
ACTION: Hang up immediately. Real banks and police never ask for OTP over the phone.

RULE P-04 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user received an email AND the sender domain does not match the official company
domain AND the email contains an attachment or link THEN risk level is HIGH.
ACTION: Do NOT open attachment or click link. Report to IT department (if corporate).

RULE P-05 [MEDIUM RISK]:
IF user received a message claiming they won a prize or are eligible for a refund
AND the message asks them to click a link to claim THEN risk level is MEDIUM-HIGH.
ACTION: Verify independently. Search the company's official website. Do not click.

RULE P-06 [MEDIUM RISK]:
IF a website is asking for login credentials AND the URL uses HTTP (not HTTPS)
OR the URL does not match the official domain THEN risk level is HIGH.
ACTION: Close the website. Do not enter any credentials.

RULE P-07 [LOW RISK - AWARENESS]:
IF user received a suspicious-looking email but has NOT clicked any link or entered
any information THEN risk level is LOW.
ACTION: Delete the email. Mark as spam. No immediate financial risk.

RULE P-08 [CRITICAL - POST-INCIDENT]:
IF user has already entered banking credentials or OTP on a suspicious website
THEN risk level is CRITICAL — potential account compromise.
ACTION: Call bank immediately to freeze account. Change all passwords. Report to PDRM.

SECTION 6: WHAT TO DO IF YOU ARE A VICTIM
================================================================================

IMMEDIATE ACTIONS (Within the first hour):
1. Call your bank's 24-hour fraud hotline immediately to freeze your account.
   - Maybank: 1-300-88-6688
   - CIMB: 1-300-880-900
   - Public Bank: 1-800-22-5555
   - RHB: 1-800-88-9950
   - Hong Leong: 1-300-88-8111
   - AmBank: 1-800-88-9999
   - BSN: 1-300-88-1900
   OR call NSRC (National Scam Response Centre): 997

2. Change all passwords immediately — email, banking, social media.
   Use a different device if you suspect your device is compromised.

3. Enable two-factor authentication on all important accounts.

4. Do NOT share the incident with scammers or respond to follow-up messages.

REPORTING:
- PDRM Cybercrime: Report at ccid.rmp.gov.my or call 03-2610-1559
- MCMC: aduan.mcmc.gov.my (for website/online scam reporting)
- MyCERT: mycert.org.my/ar/aduan (for cybersecurity incidents)
- BNM BNMTELELINK: 1-300-88-5465 (for banking-related fraud)
- NSRC 997: Call 997 (fastest way to freeze mule accounts)

EVIDENCE TO PRESERVE:
- Screenshot the suspicious message, link, or email
- Note the date, time, and sender details
- Do NOT delete messages — they are needed for police investigation
- Get transaction reference numbers for any unauthorised transfers

SECTION 7: HOW TO VERIFY IF A WEBSITE IS LEGITIMATE
================================================================================

CHECK 1 - URL INSPECTION:
Look at the full URL carefully before entering any information.
Official Malaysian government websites end in .gov.my
Official bank websites: maybank2u.com.my, cimbclicks.com.my, pbebank.com, rhbgroup.com
If unsure, go directly to Google and search for the official website.

CHECK 2 - SSL CERTIFICATE:
Click the padlock icon next to the URL in your browser.
Check if the certificate is issued to the correct organisation.
HTTPS does not guarantee the site is legitimate — scammers can also use HTTPS.
But missing HTTPS (HTTP only) is always a red flag.

CHECK 3 - CONTACT VERIFICATION:
Call the organisation using the phone number listed on their official website.
Do NOT use the phone number provided in the suspicious message.
Banks will never ask you to verify your details via a link sent by SMS or email.

CHECK 4 - SEMAK MULE:
Visit semak.rmp.gov.my to check if a phone number or bank account is linked to scam reports.

CHECK 5 - WHOIS LOOKUP:
Go to whois.domaintools.com and enter the domain.
Legitimate companies own domains registered years ago.
A domain registered only days or weeks ago is a strong scam indicator.

SECTION 8: PHISHING PREVENTION TIPS
================================================================================

PREVENTION TIP 1 - NEVER SHARE OTP:
Your OTP (One-Time Password) is personal and confidential. No legitimate bank,
government agency, or company will EVER ask you for your OTP over the phone,
SMS, or email. If anyone asks for your OTP — it is a scam.

PREVENTION TIP 2 - THINK BEFORE YOU CLICK:
Hover over links before clicking to preview the actual URL.
When in doubt, type the official URL directly into your browser.
Bookmark important websites so you always visit the correct one.

PREVENTION TIP 3 - KEEP SOFTWARE UPDATED:
Enable automatic updates for your operating system, browser, and antivirus.
Outdated software has security vulnerabilities that phishers exploit.

PREVENTION TIP 4 - USE OFFICIAL APPS:
Download banking apps only from official Google Play Store or Apple App Store.
Do not install APK files sent via WhatsApp or Telegram.

PREVENTION TIP 5 - ENABLE TRANSACTION ALERTS:
Turn on SMS or email alerts for all bank transactions.
This allows you to detect unauthorised transactions immediately.

PREVENTION TIP 6 - USE A PASSWORD MANAGER:
A password manager only auto-fills credentials on the correct domain.
If you are on a phishing site, the password manager will NOT auto-fill —
this is a useful signal that the site may be fake.

PREVENTION TIP 7 - VERIFY BEFORE ACTING:
If you receive an unexpected message from your bank or government agency,
always call them directly using their official hotline before taking any action.
