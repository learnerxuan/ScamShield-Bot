SECTION 1: WHAT IS A FAKE BANK ALERT SCAM?
================================================================================

A fake bank alert scam is a type of fraud where scammers impersonate banks or
financial institutions to trick victims into revealing banking credentials, OTPs,
or personal information. The scammer's ultimate goal is to gain unauthorised access
to the victim's bank account and steal money.

In Malaysia, BNM (Bank Negara Malaysia) and AIBIM (Association of Islamic Banking
and Financial Institutions Malaysia) have issued multiple public advisories about
this type of scam. Malaysian banks most commonly impersonated include:

- Maybank / Maybank2u
- CIMB / CIMB Clicks
- Public Bank / PBe
- RHB Bank
- Hong Leong Bank
- AmBank
- Bank Islam
- Bank Rakyat
- BSN (Bank Simpanan Nasional)
- Alliance Bank

This scam is particularly dangerous because victims receive messages that look
extremely convincing — using real bank logos, official-sounding language, and
spoofed sender IDs that show the legitimate bank name.

SECTION 2: HOW FAKE BANK ALERT SCAMS WORK (STEP BY STEP)
================================================================================

STEP 1 - FAKE SECURITY ALERT
Victim receives an SMS, WhatsApp, or email that appears to be from their bank.
The message creates immediate alarm:
- "Unusual activity detected on your account"
- "Your Maybank2u account has been temporarily suspended"
- "A new device has been added to your account — verify now to prevent loss"
- "Suspicious transaction of RM3,500 detected — click here to dispute"

STEP 2 - URGENT CALL TO ACTION
The message includes a link, phone number, or instructions telling the victim to
act immediately to "secure" their account. Victim is told they will lose money
or access to their account if they do not respond within a short time window
(e.g., "within 2 hours").

STEP 3 - CREDENTIAL CAPTURE
Victim clicks the link to a fake bank login page that looks identical to the
real bank website. Victim enters their username and password.
The scammer captures the credentials in real time.

STEP 4 - OTP INTERCEPTION
The fake website then asks for an OTP (One-Time Password) sent to victim's phone,
claiming it is needed to "verify identity" or "dispute the transaction."
When victim enters the OTP, the scammer uses it to log into the real bank account.

OR — the victim calls the fake hotline number in the message, and a scammer
posing as a bank officer asks for OTP, account number, and PIN over the phone.

STEP 5 - ACCOUNT TAKEOVER AND FUND TRANSFER
With full credentials and OTP, the scammer logs into the real bank account.
They transfer funds to mule accounts as quickly as possible.
Victims lose anywhere from a few hundred ringgit to hundreds of thousands of ringgit.

STEP 6 - SECOND-STAGE SCAM (SOMETIMES)
After the initial theft, scammers may contact the victim again:
- Posing as a "fraud recovery specialist" offering to help retrieve lost funds
- Posing as a police officer investigating the case
- Both variants attempt to steal even more money from the same victim.

SECTION 3: TYPES OF FAKE BANK ALERT SCAMS
================================================================================

TYPE 1 - FAKE SUSPENSION/SECURITY ALERT SMS (Most Common)
Victim receives SMS saying account is suspended or compromised. Message contains
a link to a fake login page. Sender ID may appear as the real bank name due to
SMS sender ID spoofing.
Key identifier: Real banks never include login links in security alert SMS.

TYPE 2 - FAKE BANK OFFICER PHONE CALL
Caller claims to be a bank officer and says suspicious activity was detected.
They ask for OTP, PIN, and full account details "for verification purposes."
Caller may be very professional and convincing.
Key identifier: No legitimate bank officer ever asks for OTP over the phone.

TYPE 3 - FAKE BANK EMAIL PHISHING
Victim receives an official-looking email from a spoofed bank address.
Email contains a link to a fake bank portal. Design and branding looks authentic.
Key identifier: Hover over the sender address — domain will not match official bank.

TYPE 4 - FAKE TRANSACTION DISPUTE
Victim is told a large transaction was made from their account and to dispute it
by clicking a link or calling a number. The urgency of a large "unauthorised"
transaction pressures the victim to act without thinking.
Key identifier: Log into your real banking app to check — if no such transaction
exists, the alert is fake.

TYPE 5 - CARD BLOCKING SCAM
Victim is told their bank card has been blocked due to "suspicious activity" and
must verify details to reactivate it. Scammer collects full card number, expiry
date, and CVV — enough to make fraudulent online purchases.
Key identifier: Banks never ask for full card details over the phone to unblock a card.

TYPE 6 - BANKING APP UPGRADE SCAM
Victim is told their banking app is outdated and needs to be upgraded via a link.
The link downloads a fake app (malware/RAT) that captures SMS OTPs and credentials.
Key identifier: Never update banking apps via links in messages — use official stores only.

TYPE 7 - FAKE E-WALLET ALERT
Similar to bank alerts but targeting e-wallet users (Touch 'n Go, GrabPay, Boost,
BigPay). Victim is told their e-wallet has been compromised and must verify.
Key identifier: Same warning signs apply — do not click links in e-wallet alert messages.

SECTION 4: SENDER ID SPOOFING — WHY FAKE ALERTS LOOK REAL
================================================================================

A particularly deceptive technique used in bank alert scams is SMS Sender ID Spoofing.

In Malaysia and globally, SMS technology allows the sender to replace a phone number
with a text name (e.g., "Maybank" or "CIMB-Bank"). This technology is designed for
legitimate business use, but scammers exploit it to make their fraudulent SMS appear
in the SAME conversation thread as real bank messages on your phone.

What this means: You may see a scam SMS appearing in the same thread as legitimate
Maybank SMS messages. The SMS thread label will say "Maybank" even though it came
from scammers. Your phone does NOT verify the identity of the sender.

Malaysian banks have been working with telcos and MCMC to combat sender ID spoofing,
but it remains an active threat.

How to detect spoofed bank SMS:
- A real bank SMS for security alerts will NOT contain a clickable link
- Real security alerts instruct you to call the number on the BACK of your bank card
- Real banks do NOT send SMS saying "your account is suspended — click here to fix"
- If unsure: open your banking app directly or call the number on your card

SECTION 5: RED FLAGS AND WARNING SIGNS
================================================================================

IN THE MESSAGE CONTENT:
- Urgent language: "immediate action required", "account suspended", "within 2 hours"
- A link in a security alert SMS from your bank
- Message mentions a large transaction you do not recall making
- Message asks you to call a specific phone number provided in the SMS
- Grammar errors, unusual phrasing, or inconsistent bank branding
- Message mentions your account will be closed if you do not respond

IN THE LINK OR WEBSITE:
- URL does not match official bank domain exactly
  e.g., maybank2u-secure.com / maybonk.com / maybank-my.net (all FAKE)
  Official Maybank: maybank2u.com.my
- Website URL uses HTTP (not HTTPS) — missing padlock
- Login page design has minor differences (fonts, colours, logo positioning)
- After logging in, site immediately asks for OTP — real bank would show your actual
  account summary first
- Site asks for more information than usual (e.g., full card number, mother's maiden name)

ON THE PHONE:
- Caller claims to be a bank officer and knows your name and last 4 digits of your card
  (scammers buy data from data breaches — knowing your name does NOT mean they are real)
- Caller asks you to share your OTP to "verify your identity" or "stop the transaction"
- Caller asks you to stay on the line and not end the call (high pressure tactic)
- Caller asks you to transfer money to a "safe account" to protect your funds
- You are transferred to multiple "departments" or "officers" during the call

SECTION 6: RISK ASSESSMENT RULES (IF-THEN PRODUCTION RULES)
================================================================================

RULE BA-01 [CRITICAL RISK]:
IF user received a call from someone claiming to be a bank officer AND they are
asking for OTP, PIN, or password THEN risk level is CRITICAL.
ACTION: Hang up immediately. No bank officer will ever ask for your OTP.
Call back the bank using the number on the back of your card.

RULE BA-02 [CRITICAL RISK]:
IF user has already provided their OTP to a caller or a website THEN risk level
is CRITICAL — account may already be compromised.
ACTION: Call bank fraud hotline IMMEDIATELY to freeze account. Call NSRC 997.
Check transaction history for unauthorised transfers.

RULE BA-03 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user received an SMS from "bank name" AND the SMS contains a link to click
for security purposes THEN risk level is HIGH.
ACTION: Do NOT click. Real banks do not send clickable links in security SMS.
Verify by logging into your banking app directly or calling bank hotline.

RULE BA-04 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user is on a website that looks like a bank login page AND the URL does not
exactly match the official bank domain THEN risk level is HIGH.
ACTION: Do NOT enter credentials. Close the browser tab. Go to the official URL.

RULE BA-05 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user received a message saying their bank account is suspended AND message
provides a phone number to call that is different from the number on their bank card
THEN risk level is HIGH.
ACTION: Do NOT call the number in the message. Call the official number on your card.

RULE BA-06 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user is asked to transfer money to a "safe account" to protect from fraud
THEN risk level is CRITICAL. This is always a scam.
ACTION: No bank will ever ask you to transfer to a safe account. Refuse and call
the real bank immediately.

RULE BA-07 [MEDIUM RISK]:
IF user received a suspicious bank alert email AND has NOT clicked any link or
entered any information THEN risk level is LOW-MEDIUM.
ACTION: Delete the email. Log into your bank account directly to check status.
Report the phishing email to your bank.

RULE BA-08 [MEDIUM RISK]:
IF user installed a banking app from a link in a message (not from Play Store/App Store)
THEN risk level is HIGH.
ACTION: Uninstall immediately. Factory reset device. Contact bank to freeze account.

SECTION 7: HOW TO TELL IF YOUR BANK ACTUALLY CONTACTED YOU
================================================================================

REAL BANK ALERTS — What Banks Actually Do:
- Real bank OTP SMS contains only the OTP number and a brief description
- Real bank security alerts instruct you to call the number on your card, not a new number
- Real bank emails come from official domain (e.g., @maybank.com.my, @cimb.com)
- Real bank apps never ask for OTP via a link — OTP arrives via SMS separately
- Real banks never ask you to share your OTP with anyone, including bank staff

HOW TO VERIFY:
1. Do NOT use the link, email, or number provided in the suspicious message
2. Open your banking app directly on your phone
3. Check your transaction history — if the "suspicious transaction" does not appear,
   the alert is fake
4. Call the bank using the number printed on the back of your physical bank card
5. Visit the nearest bank branch in person if you are unsure

CHECKING OFFICIAL BANK HOTLINES (Malaysia):

SECTION 8: WHAT TO DO IF YOU ARE A VICTIM
================================================================================

IMMEDIATE ACTIONS — FIRST 30 MINUTES ARE CRITICAL:
1. Call your bank's 24-hour fraud hotline from the number on your bank card
   (not from any number provided in suspicious messages)
2. Ask the bank to: freeze your account, block online transactions, flag the case
3. Call NSRC 997 — they can coordinate with banks to freeze mule accounts before
   the scammer withdraws the money
4. Change your online banking password and PIN from a clean, uncompromised device

REPORTING:
- PDRM (police report): ccid.rmp.gov.my or nearest police station
- BNM BNMTELELINK: 1-300-88-5465 (for unresolved disputes with banks)
- MCMC: aduan.mcmc.gov.my (if fraud involved SMS or online communication)
- NSRC 997 (fastest response for freezing mule accounts)

EVIDENCE TO PRESERVE:
- Screenshot all suspicious messages (SMS, WhatsApp, email)
- Screenshot the fake website URL
- Screenshot the fake transaction history (if visible on the fake site)
- Write down the caller's phone number, name used, time of call
- Get your bank's reference number for the fraud report
- All evidence is needed for police investigation and bank dispute process

BANK DISPUTE PROCESS:
- File a formal dispute with your bank in writing
- Banks are required under BNM guidelines to investigate and respond within 14 days
- If unsatisfied with bank's response, escalate to BNM BNMTELELINK: 1-300-88-5465
- For legal action: consult a lawyer about potential claims

SECTION 9: PREVENTION — HOW TO PROTECT YOURSELF
================================================================================

PREVENTION TIP 1 - NEVER SHARE OTP:
OTP is a One-Time Password for YOUR use only. No bank staff, police officer,
or government agent is ever authorised to ask you for it.

PREVENTION TIP 2 - VERIFY BEFORE ACTING:
If you receive any security alert, verify by logging into your official banking app
directly — not by clicking the link in the message.

PREVENTION TIP 3 - REGISTER FOR TRANSACTION ALERTS:
Enable real-time SMS or push notification alerts for all transactions above RM1.
This lets you detect unauthorised transactions the moment they occur.

PREVENTION TIP 4 - REVIEW TRANSACTIONS REGULARLY:
Check your bank statement at least once a week. Early detection limits damage.

PREVENTION TIP 5 - SET DAILY TRANSFER LIMITS:
Log into your real banking app and set a low daily online transfer limit.
This limits the maximum amount a scammer can steal even if they gain access.

PREVENTION TIP 6 - USE SECURE KILL SWITCH:
Maybank and some other banks offer a "kill switch" or account freeze feature
directly in the app that lets you instantly block all transactions if you
suspect fraud — activate this immediately if you receive suspicious activity.

PREVENTION TIP 7 - USE OFFICIAL APP FROM OFFICIAL STORE:
Download your banking app only from the official Google Play Store or Apple App Store.
Enable automatic app updates to ensure you have the latest security patches.
