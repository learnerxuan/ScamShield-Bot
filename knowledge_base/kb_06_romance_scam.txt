SECTION 1: WHAT IS A ROMANCE SCAM?
================================================================================

A romance scam (also called catfishing or love scam) is a type of fraud where
criminals create fake romantic relationships to emotionally manipulate victims
into sending money, gifts, cryptocurrency, or personal information.

The scammer poses as an attractive, successful, and caring person — often a
foreign professional (doctor, military officer, engineer working abroad, or
offshore oil rig worker). After building a deep emotional bond over weeks or
months, they invent financial emergencies and ask the victim for help.

Romance scams are among the most emotionally devastating types of fraud because:
- Victims believe they are in a genuine relationship and have made an emotional bond
- Many victims feel shame and do not report or seek help
- Scammers may continue contacting victims even after the scam is exposed
- The financial losses can be catastrophic — from thousands to millions of ringgit

In Malaysia, PDRM and MCMC report romance scams as one of the top three most
reported cybercrime categories. Victims include all genders and ages, though
statistics show middle-aged adults and seniors are disproportionately targeted.

SECTION 2: HOW ROMANCE SCAMS WORK (STEP BY STEP)
================================================================================

STEP 1 - PROFILE CREATION (CATFISHING)
Scammer creates a convincing fake identity using stolen photos from real people
(often military personnel, doctors, engineers, or attractive models).
They create complete social media profiles — Facebook, Instagram, LinkedIn —
with years of posts, tagged photos, and friends.

STEP 2 - INITIAL CONTACT
Scammer contacts victim via:
- Facebook (most common in Malaysia)
- Instagram, Twitter/X
- Dating apps: Tinder, Bumble, Badoo, Tagged
- WhatsApp (wrong number opener)
- LinkedIn (poses as a business professional)
- In some cases, through mutual "friends" (the friend's account was hacked)

Common opening gambits:
- "Hi, I think I know you from somewhere — are you from [city]?"
- Sending a message to a "wrong number" and then striking up conversation
- Leaving a comment on victim's social media post

STEP 3 - RELATIONSHIP DEVELOPMENT
Scammer invests significant time building a deep, personal relationship:
- Daily messages — good morning and good night texts
- Expressing interest in victim's life, children, work, hobbies
- Sharing (fabricated) personal stories to build intimacy and trust
- Quick declarations of love or deep connection ("soulmate", "the one")
- Discusses plans for the future — meeting in person, marriage, travel together

The scammer is typically portrayed as:
- A widower or divorcee (often with one young child who needs a maternal/paternal figure)
- Working abroad — oil rig, military deployment, construction in Middle East
- Extremely successful — doctor, engineer, architect, military general

STEP 4 - CRISIS AND MONEY REQUEST
After weeks or months, a financial emergency arises:
- Medical emergency ("I had an accident — I need money for surgery")
- Business deal going wrong ("My shipment is stuck in customs — I need RM10,000")
- Airline ticket to finally meet ("My company owes me money but I need bridge funding")
- Investment opportunity — victim invited to participate in a "sure-win" deal
- Legal trouble — scammer arrested and needs bail money or lawyer fees

The request escalates: each time the victim pays, a new emergency appears.
The scammer is emotionally manipulative — guilt-tripping, declaring love, and
threatening to end the relationship if the victim does not help.

STEP 5 - FINANCIAL EXPLOITATION
Money is sent via:
- Bank transfer to "mule" accounts
- Gift cards (iTunes, Google Play, Steam, Amazon) — untraceable
- Cryptocurrency (Bitcoin, USDT)
- Wire transfers overseas
- Online payment platforms

STEP 6 - DISAPPEARANCE OR ESCALATION
When the victim has no more money to give or becomes suspicious, the scammer:
- Disappears completely (cuts all contact)
- Becomes aggressive, threatening legal action or releasing private information
- Claims to have been "robbed" or "arrested" — requesting rescue money
- Attempts to sell the victim a "recovery scam" — offering to help retrieve lost funds
  for a fee (all paid to the same criminal syndicate)

SECTION 3: TYPES OF ROMANCE SCAMS
================================================================================

TYPE 1 - CLASSIC CATFISH ROMANCE SCAM
Long-term fake relationship built over months. Gradual financial requests. Victim
genuinely believes in the relationship and is emotionally invested before money is requested.
Key identifier: Person is always unavailable to video call "live" — camera is broken,
deployed in a remote area, or quality is always too poor to confirm identity.

TYPE 2 - PIG BUTCHERING ROMANCE SCAM (Sha Zhu Pan - Love + Investment)
Romantic relationship is used as a vehicle to introduce an investment opportunity.
After building emotional trust, scammer introduces a "secret" investment platform
where victim can "make money together."
Initial small withdrawals allowed to build confidence. Then victim is encouraged
to invest larger amounts until the platform freezes and everything is lost.
Key identifier: New romantic interest who introduces an investment platform
and claims personal financial expertise.

TYPE 3 - MILITARY ROMANCE SCAM
Scammer poses as a military officer deployed overseas (US Army, UN peacekeeping forces).
Claims unable to access military pay — needs help with bills, medical costs, or travel.
Claims to be due a large pension soon — victim just needs to pay small fees first.
Key identifier: Real military personnel on deployment have access to financial services.
Legitimate soldiers do not request money from online romantic partners.

TYPE 4 - DOCTOR / PROFESSIONAL ROMANCE SCAM
Scammer claims to be a doctor, engineer, or other professional working abroad.
Establishes credibility through technical language and professional references.
Romantic interest is used to build trust before financial requests begin.
Key identifier: Professional who is always working in a remote or dangerous location.

TYPE 5 - INHERITANCE / BUSINESS DEAL SCAM VARIANT
Scammer is a "wealthy" individual who shares a business opportunity or inheritance
story — victim is asked to help with small fees to release large sums.
Similar to advance fee fraud (419 scam) but delivered through a romantic channel.
Key identifier: Promises of sharing large sums of money once victim helps with fees.

TYPE 6 - SEXTORTION ROMANCE SCAM
Scammer builds romantic relationship and pressures victim into sharing intimate
photos or participating in explicit video calls. Footage is then used to blackmail
the victim for money, threatening to send the content to family or employers.
Key identifier: New online relationship that quickly becomes sexual. Requests for
intimate content from someone never met in person.

SECTION 4: RED FLAGS AND WARNING SIGNS
================================================================================

PROFILE RED FLAGS:
- Profile photos look like professional model shots or stock photos
- Very few photos available — photos all look taken in professional settings
- Profile was created recently (few months old) with few friends or connections
- Profile shows a person who is exceptionally attractive, successful, and single
- Reverse image search (Google Images, TinEye) shows photos belong to another person

COMMUNICATION RED FLAGS:
- Contacts you without any prior connection — "wrong number" or random message
- Falls in love or develops deep affection very quickly (within days or weeks)
- Communicates predominantly via WhatsApp or Telegram, avoids video calls
- When video calls happen, quality is always poor — camera "broken" or "connection bad"
- Messages feel scripted or copy-paste — responses do not address specific questions
- Language is sometimes inconsistent — unusual grammar or phrases despite claiming
  to be a native English or Malay speaker
- Avoids specific personal details, changes subject when pressed for verification

RELATIONSHIP RED FLAGS:
- Works in a remote location: military deployment, offshore oil rig, UN mission,
  mining site, ship at sea
- Always prevented from meeting in person — flights cancelled, visa problems, work delays
- Is a widow/widower with a young child who needs care
- Claims to be extremely wealthy or successful but needs financial help
- Asks you to communicate ONLY via private channels — not to tell family or friends

MONEY REQUEST RED FLAGS:
- Any request for money from someone you have never met in person
- Emergency that only money can solve — medical, legal, business, travel
- Request for gift cards (iTunes, Google Play, Steam, Amazon) — untraceable
- Request for cryptocurrency transfer
- Offers to send you money first — then asks you to forward it (money mule setup)
- Promises that you will receive a large sum of money once you help with a fee
- Multiple emergencies over time — each resolved by sending more money

SECTION 5: RISK ASSESSMENT RULES (IF-THEN PRODUCTION RULES)
================================================================================

RULE RS-01 [CRITICAL RISK]:
IF user has sent money to an online romantic partner AND has never met them in
person AND the person continues to ask for more money THEN risk level is CRITICAL.
ACTION: Stop sending money immediately. Contact PDRM and NSRC 997.
Speak to a trusted family member or friend. Do not be ashamed — you are a victim.

RULE RS-02 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user is in an online relationship AND the person has never agreed to a live,
unscripted video call AND has started asking for money THEN risk level is HIGH.
ACTION: Demand a live video call with a specific requested action (wave hand,
show a piece of paper with today's date). If refused, this person is likely fake.

RULE RS-03 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user wants to send money to someone they met online AND has not met in person
AND the person's photos return different results on reverse image search
THEN risk level is CRITICAL.
ACTION: Stop all financial transactions. This person is using stolen photos.

RULE RS-04 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user's new online romantic partner has introduced them to an investment platform
and encouraged them to invest THEN risk level is HIGH — Pig Butchering pattern.
ACTION: Stop investing. Do not make further deposits. This is likely a romance-
investment hybrid scam designed to steal all deposited funds.

RULE RS-05 [HIGH RISK]:
IF user's new romantic partner is asking for iTunes gift cards, Google Play cards,
Steam cards, or Amazon gift cards for any reason THEN risk level is CRITICAL.
ACTION: Reject the request. Gift cards are the payment method of choice for scammers
because they are untraceable. No legitimate romantic partner asks for gift cards.

RULE RS-06 [MEDIUM RISK]:
IF user met someone online recently AND the person declares love or deep affection
very quickly AND the person claims to work in a remote location preventing meeting
THEN risk level is MEDIUM — early warning signs present.
ACTION: Advise caution. Perform reverse image search on profile photos.
Do not send money. Discuss with a trusted person before proceeding.

RULE RS-07 [MEDIUM RISK]:
IF user received intimate images or participated in explicit video call AND is now
being threatened or pressured for money to keep content private
THEN risk level is CRITICAL — sextortion.
ACTION: Do NOT pay. Payment often increases demands. Report to PDRM CyberSafe
and MCMC. Save all evidence (screenshots of threats).

SECTION 6: HOW TO VERIFY A PERSON'S IDENTITY ONLINE
================================================================================

STEP 1 - REVERSE IMAGE SEARCH:
Right-click on the profile photo and select "Search image" (in Google Chrome).
Or go to images.google.com and drag the photo into the search box.
Or use TinEye (tineye.com) for a more thorough reverse search.
If the same photo appears with a different name or on stock photo sites, it is stolen.

STEP 2 - REQUEST A LIVE VIDEO CALL WITH VERIFICATION:
Ask the person to wave their hand, write your name on paper and hold it up, or
perform a specific action on the live video call. Scammers cannot manipulate
pre-recorded videos to perform specific real-time requests.
If they always have excuses for why they cannot video call — be suspicious.

STEP 3 - VERIFY CLAIMED IDENTITY:
If person claims to work for a specific hospital, company, or military unit,
look up the organisation independently and call them to verify the person's
employment. Do not use contact details provided by the person.

STEP 4 - SEARCH NAME + SCAM:
Google the person's name and the word "scam" or "romance scam" or "catfish".
Other victims may have already reported and documented the same fake persona.

STEP 5 - SEMAK MULE:
If the person has given you a Malaysian bank account to send money to,
check it at semak.rmp.gov.my. The account may already be flagged as a mule account.

STEP 6 - ASK TRUSTED FRIENDS OR FAMILY:
Share the person's profile and story with someone you trust. Romance scam victims
often become emotionally blinded and cannot see the red flags clearly.
An outside perspective is often very helpful.

SECTION 7: THE EMOTIONAL IMPACT AND HOW TO COPE
================================================================================

Romance scams cause deep emotional trauma beyond financial loss:
- Grief and loss — victim had genuine feelings for a relationship that was fake
- Shame and embarrassment — preventing victims from seeking help or reporting
- Depression and anxiety — especially when large sums were lost
- Trust issues — difficulty trusting new relationships
- Isolation — many victims do not tell family and withdraw socially

It is important to know:
- Being scammed does NOT mean you are stupid or naive — these criminals are professional
  psychological manipulators. They study human psychology and know how to exploit trust.
- You are NOT alone — thousands of Malaysians experience romance scams every year.
- Reporting is important — it helps prevent the same criminal from victimising others.
- Recovery is possible — emotionally and financially.

Where to get support:
- Befrienders Malaysia (emotional support): 03-7627-2929 (24-hour helpline)
- Malaysian Mental Health Association: mmha.org.my | 03-2780-6803
- Talian Kasih (family/domestic issues): 15999 | WhatsApp 019-261-5999

SECTION 8: WHAT TO DO IF YOU ARE A VICTIM
================================================================================

IMMEDIATE STEPS:
1. Stop all communication with the scammer
2. Stop all money transfers — even if the scammer threatens consequences
3. Block the scammer on all platforms (WhatsApp, Facebook, Instagram, Telegram)
4. Call NSRC 997 if money was transferred recently — they can attempt to freeze accounts
5. Talk to a trusted person — family member, close friend, or counsellor

REPORTING:
- PDRM (CCID): ccid.rmp.gov.my | 03-2610-1559
- MCMC: aduan.mcmc.gov.my | 1-800-188-030
- NSRC: 997
- Report fake profile to the social media platform (Facebook, Instagram)

EVIDENCE TO PRESERVE:
- Screenshots of all conversations (WhatsApp, Facebook Messenger, etc.)
- Profile photos, name, username of the scammer
- Bank transfer receipts and reference numbers
- Any phone numbers or email addresses used
- Screenshots of the payment requests
- All evidence needed for PDRM investigation

FINANCIAL RECOVERY:
- Contact your bank immediately — if transfer was recent, freezing the mule account
  may be possible
- File a formal police report — necessary for bank dispute and legal action
- Consult AKPK for debt counselling if you took loans or used credit: 1-800-88-2575
